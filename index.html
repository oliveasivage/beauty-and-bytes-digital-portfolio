The story behind the study

This project started with a retail truth: foundation is not just another product. It is personal, visible, and deeply tied to customer confidence. That makes customer feedback one of the richest but most underused data sources in beauty.

From storefront observation to analytics question

Through beauty retail experience, I saw how customers could leave disappointed even after doing everything right. Sometimes the issue was not application technique or product knowledge. The issue was that their undertone, shade depth, or skin condition was not being served consistently.

That observation became an analytics problem. Customer reviews were full of clues about shade match, wear, comfort, oxidation, coverage, and skin compatibility, but those clues were buried inside unstructured text and dynamic product pages. This capstone turns that messy feedback into a structured analytical asset.

The analytics pipeline

Reliable insight starts with reliable structure. This capstone emphasizes the end-to-end workflow, not just the final charts.

How the data was built

Sephora review content lives inside dynamic interfaces, so static scraping alone would not capture the full story. I used Python and Playwright to automate browsing behavior, load review sections, interact with buttons, and extract review-level detail into a structured dataset.

Cleaning and validation

Raw scraped data included duplicates, empty entries, and non-review content such as Q&A text. Those issues were addressed by filtering non-meaningful records, removing duplicate reviews by text matching, converting ratings to numeric values, and standardizing skin-type and skin-tone categories.

Tools used

Python, Playwright, regular expressions, Pandas, and Matplotlib were used to build a modular workflow that could scale across additional products and categories. Screenshots and HTML snapshots supported debugging and pipeline reliability.

Average rating by skin type

ANOVA: p = 0.0095
Here, the variation is more meaningful. At least one skin-type group experiences significantly different satisfaction.

While the ANOVA confirms that differences exist across skin types, it does not show exactly where those differences occur. A follow-up Tukey test revealed that the Normal skin type differs significantly from Combination, Dry, and Oily skin types, while most other comparisons were not statistically significant. This suggests that the variation in ratings is largely driven by differences involving Normal skin users.

What the customer voice reveals

Numbers show where to look. Text tells us why. Together, they create the full customer experience story.

Insight 01

Shade inclusivity is not the whole story

Strong results across skin tones suggest that shade diversity has improved. But customers do not only evaluate whether they found a shade. They also evaluate how that shade wears, blends, and lasts on their specific skin.

Insight 02

Variance matters more than averages

High mean ratings can hide uneven experiences inside a group. In beauty analytics, variance is valuable because it can reveal which customers are being served inconsistently even when the top-line story looks positive.

Insight 03

Formulation may be the next frontier

The strongest signal in this analysis is not a broad shade failure. It is the statistically significant variation across skin types, which suggests formula performance deserves just as much attention as shade range.

Key conclusion

The strongest business story from this capstone is nuanced. Sephora foundation products appear to perform relatively consistently across skin tones, which supports the idea that shade inclusivity efforts are having an effect. However, that same consistency does not fully extend across skin types. This variation is primarily driven by differences involving Normal skin users.
